## What actually drives cost
Headline rent is the number everyone stares at, but the *net effective rent* — headline rent minus the value of all incentives, spread over the term to the first break — is what a tenant actually pays. A landlord will happily hold headline rent (it protects their asset valuation and the yield they report to investors) while giving away the equivalent value in rent-free months, fit-out cash, and service-charge concessions that never show in the headline. **Negotiate net effective, not headline.** Second cost driver: the *repairing and service-charge regime*, which quietly transfers the building's ageing and running costs onto the tenant. Third: *exit cost* — dilapidations and the absence of a break clause can turn a "cheap" lease into a trap.

## The key levers (concrete)
- **Rent-free / incentive period.** The most flexible landlord give — it protects headline rent and thus valuation, so ask for it *first* and generously. Split it: part as pure rent-free (fit-out period), part as a contribution to cost of works.
- **Break clause.** A tenant-only, *unconditional* break is the single most valuable flexibility term. Fight every landlord condition — "vacant possession", "all rents paid", "material compliance" are the standard sabotage conditions that let a landlord defeat a break on a technicality. Demand: only pre-conditions are rent paid to date and vacant possession of the demise; no penalty for minor disrepair.
- **Rent review.** Prefer *indexed* (CPI/VPI-linked, ideally capped-and-collared) over *open-market* review. Note jurisdiction: "upward-only" reviews are a contractual feature predominantly seen in UK leases, not intrinsic to open-market review. In Germany, Staffelmiete (fixed step) and Indexmiete (index-linked) are two common escalation mechanisms — primarily developed in residential tenancy law (§557a/§557b BGB); commercial leases have wider freedom of contract, so confirm the applicable regime live. If open-market is unavoidable, strike "upward-only". In Germany a *Staffelmiete* (fixed step) or *Indexmiete* (index-linked) are the two statutory options — know which you're being offered.
- **Service charge.** Cap the annual increase (a % or index cap), exclude improvement/capex from the cap (you shouldn't fund the landlord upgrading their asset), and secure *audit rights* plus a cap on management fees. Get a "no-surprise" clause: charges not billed within a set window are waived.
- **Repairing obligations.** In UK/common-law leases, FRI (Full Repairing & Insuring) dumps the whole building's condition on the tenant while IRI (Internal Repairing) is far better — push FRI toward IRI. In a GERMAN commercial lease (Gewerbemiete, BGB) there is no FRI/IRI; repair allocation runs through Instandhaltung/Instandsetzung clauses and Schönheitsreparaturen case law (BGH) — negotiate which repairs the tenant bears there. Either way, if broad repairing liability is forced, bind it with a **schedule of condition** (photographic record at entry) so you hand it back as-received, not as-new.
- **Dilapidations.** Cap liability to the schedule of condition; exclude fair wear and tear; ideally convert to a fixed end-of-term payment agreed up front rather than an open-ended claim.
- **Fit-out contribution & security.** Trade a larger landlord fit-out contribution against holding headline rent. On security, push rent-deposit down and resist an over-long bank guarantee — every month of guarantee ties up your credit line.

## Category traps suppliers (landlords/agents) use
- **Upward-only rent review** dressed as "market standard". It isn't a law of nature — resist it.
- **Conditional break clauses** engineered to be un-exercisable.
- **Headline-rent anchoring** while conceding only invisible incentives — you feel like you won and still overpay net.
- **Uncapped service charges** with capex smuggled in.
- **FRI + no schedule of condition** = you inherit and hand back a fully-repaired building you never damaged.

## What to trade (give / get)
Give the landlord **headline rent and term certainty** (both protect their valuation and financing) to get **net-effective concessions and flexibility**: a longer commitment *to the first break* in exchange for a bigger rent-free, a service-charge cap, and a tenant-favourable dilapidations settlement. Give a modest security deposit to get FRI softened to IRI-with-schedule.

## Mapping to the engine's terms
- **price → net effective rent** (Direction: MINIMIZE). Score the incentive-adjusted figure, not headline.
- **contract_length → lease term to first break** (the flexibility trade lives here — longer term is a *concession you sell* for price/incentive gains).
- **payment_days → rent payment frequency/timing** (monthly vs quarterly-in-advance; German commercial leases often bill quarterly in advance — pushing toward monthly-in-arrears improves working capital).
- **rebate → rent-free period + fit-out contribution** (the landlord's cheapest give; weight it heavily as your primary "get").
- **volume → demised area / additional space or renewal options** (option to expand/contract, or take-up across multiple units, as a bundling lever).
Weight *price (net effective)* and *rebate (incentives)* highest for the buyer; hold *contract_length* as the low-personal-value term you concede to a term-hungry landlord. Never let the reservation floor drop below "unconditional tenant break + capped service charge".

## Key levers

- Negotiate NET EFFECTIVE rent (headline minus incentives over term-to-break), never headline — landlords trade invisible incentives to protect headline valuation
- Extract rent-free/incentive first and hard: it's the landlord's cheapest give because it preserves headline rent and asset yield
- Win a tenant-only UNCONDITIONAL break clause; strip landlord conditions (vacant possession beyond the demise, 'all sums paid', material-compliance) that defeat it on a technicality
- Prefer indexed/capped-and-collared rent review over open-market; always strike 'upward-only'
- Cap the service charge, exclude capex/improvements from it, and secure audit rights plus a management-fee cap and a late-billing waiver window
- Downgrade FRI to IRI where possible; if FRI is forced, bind it to a photographic schedule of condition so you return as-received not as-new
- Cap dilapidations to the schedule of condition, exclude fair wear and tear, and try to fix an agreed end-of-term payment up front
- Trade term certainty and headline rent (which the landlord values for financing) to get incentives, flexibility, and a service-charge cap

## Sourcing discipline

Every quantitative figure in a live negotiation MUST be sourced, not asserted by the agent: (1) prevailing headline rent and market rent-free/incentive levels for the specific city, submarket, and asset class — pull from a live broker/valuation source (e.g. IPD/MSCI, JLL/CBRE/Colliers market reports), never quote a rent-free duration or €/m² as fact; (2) typical service-charge €/m² and management-fee percentages — market-specific, source live; (3) any statutory reference for Germany — the mechanics of Indexmiete vs Staffelmiete, permitted index (VPI/CPI), and notice/break rules under the BGB — must be confirmed against the current statute or legal counsel with a retrieval date, not stated from memory; capped-and-collared review percentages are deal-specific and unsourced here; (4) security deposit / bank-guarantee norms (number of months' rent) vary by covenant strength and market — source live; (5) the claim that open-market reviews are 'almost always upward-only' reflects a well-known market pattern but its prevalence in a given jurisdiction/period should be verified before asserting to a counterparty; (6) I did not verify current German statutory lease rules in this session — treat all legal-mechanism references as needing counsel/statute confirmation. The agent must present all such numbers as 'to be confirmed from [named source, dated]', never as established fact to the landlord.
