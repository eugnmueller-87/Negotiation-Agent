## What actually drives cost in Facility Services

FM cost is labour plus a management overlay, not "a service." In cleaning, security, catering, grounds and reception the wage bill is the dominant, largely fixed input; the negotiable margin sits in (a) how the work is *specified*, (b) the *management fee / overhead & profit* on top of pass-through labour, and (c) *risk allocation* (KPIs, indexation, scope creep). If you only push on headline rate, you compress the one part suppliers can defend as "already at cost" and leave the fee, the productivity assumptions and the risk terms untouched. Attack specification and productivity, not just the number.

## The key levers

- **Output/SLA-based spec, not input/FTE.** Insist the spec pays for a *result* (e.g. defined cleanliness/appearance standard, response times, availability) rather than a headcount roster. Input pricing hands the supplier a guaranteed FTE bill and removes their incentive to be efficient; output pricing transfers productivity risk to them and lets you cut cost through method, not just rate. Open-book on labour + a transparent management fee is the strongest structure for large contracts.
- **Price per m² (and per-service unit) benchmarking.** Normalise every bid to cost per m² per annum (cleaning), per guarding-hour (security), per cover (catering) so you can compare like-for-like and expose loaded rates. Benchmark against your own portfolio and current market data — do not accept a lump sum you can't decompose.
- **KPI / penalty regime with teeth but symmetry.** Tie a meaningful slice of the management fee (fee-at-risk) to a small number of measurable KPIs. Guard against: unmeasurable KPIs, self-reporting, caps that make failure cheap, and "gainshare" that only shares gains. Deductions must be a real remedy, not a token.
- **Bundle vs unbundle deliberately.** Bundling (IFM/TFM: cleaning + security + M&E + catering under one management layer) buys a single fee, one point of accountability and cross-service flex — but adds a margin on subcontracted trades and dampens per-line price tension. Unbundling keeps each line competitive but multiplies management. Use bundling as a *concession you grant for a price/fee cut*, not a default.
- **Scope-creep control.** The classic leak. Nail down a priced schedule of rates (SOR) / rate card for additional and reactive works, define what's in the base fee vs. chargeable, and cap uplift on variations. Without an SOR, every "extra" is sole-sourced to the incumbent at their price.
- **TUPE / staff-transfer.** On outsource or re-tender, transferring staff carry protected terms, pension and often accrued liabilities. Suppliers price a risk premium for unknown workforce data. Neutralise it: provide accurate, warranted TUPE/ELI data early so bidders price the real liability, not a padded guess — this alone can move the number.
- **Self-delivery vs subcontract.** Ask what the prime self-delivers vs subcontracts; subcontracted lines carry a second margin. Push high-volume trades to self-delivery or to your directly-managed subs to strip the layer.
- **Benchmarking & indexation clauses.** Multi-year deals must include a right to benchmark/market-test mid-term and to re-open pricing, plus a *defined* indexation index (a specified labour/wage index, not "supplier's discretion"). Cap indexation and tie any statutory wage rise to evidence.
- **Energy / consumables pass-through.** For energy-touching services (M&E, catering utilities, cleaning consumables) demand pass-through at cost with open invoices — never a margin on energy — and no minimum consumption guarantees.

## Category-specific traps suppliers use

- Bidding a low mobilisation/year-1 rate, then recovering it through variations and indexation ("buy the contract, win it back on scope").
- FTE/input pricing dressed as "quality assurance" so productivity risk stays with you.
- KPI regimes with fee-at-risk that never bites; self-scored performance.
- Vague TUPE assumptions priced as a premium that never unwinds.
- Bundling everything to obscure line-level margin and lock in incumbency.
- Uncapped, index-linked annual uplifts with no benchmarking right.

## What to trade (give a low-value term to get a high-value one)

- **Give contract length → get lower price/fee.** Longer term lets the supplier amortise mobilisation and TUPE risk; extract a real rate cut and a benchmarking/break clause in return. Never give length without the benchmarking right.
- **Give volume/scope consolidation (bundling) → get management-fee reduction and gainshare.** Their efficiency gain, your fee cut.
- **Give faster payment days → get price or rebate.** Improved cash flow has genuine value to labour-heavy suppliers; trade DPO reduction for a volume rebate or unit-rate cut.
- **Give committed volume → get a rebate/tiered pricing.** Turn spend certainty into a retro rebate rather than a headline discount you can't verify.

## Mapping to the engine's terms

- **price** = the unit rate (per m² / per hour / per cover) *and* the management fee %. Prioritise fee and productivity assumptions over headline rate.
- **payment_days** = DPO; a low-weight give-away to trade for price or rebate.
- **contract_length** = amortisation lever; concede length only bundled with a benchmarking/market-test right and a break clause.
- **volume** = committed scope/estate; convert to rebate, not just discount.
- **rebate** = the retro/gainshare mechanism where KPI performance, volume commitment and open-book savings are returned to you.


## Key levers

- Pay for an output/SLA standard, not FTE headcount — transfer productivity risk to the supplier via open-book labour + transparent management fee
- Decompose every bid to cost per m² / per guarding-hour / per cover and benchmark against portfolio and live market data — reject undecomposable lump sums
- Put a meaningful management-fee-at-risk against 3-5 measurable, independently-verified KPIs; ban self-scoring and toothless caps
- Treat bundling (IFM/TFM) as a concession you grant for a fee cut, not a default — unbundle to keep line-level price tension
- Lock a priced schedule of rates for reactive/additional works and cap variation uplift to kill scope creep
- Provide accurate, warranted TUPE/employee-liability data early so bidders price the real liability instead of a padded risk premium
- Require a mid-term benchmarking / market-test right and a defined, capped indexation index on any multi-year deal
- Force energy and consumables to at-cost pass-through with open invoices and no minimum-consumption guarantee

## Sourcing discipline

The playbook deliberately contains NO invented figures. Every quantitative claim a buyer would want — subcontract/agency markup %, typical management-fee %, market cost per m² for cleaning, guarding hourly rates, catering cost per cover, prevailing wage/labour indexation rates, and fee-at-risk percentages — must be sourced live at negotiation time ("benchmark the current market markup / rate"), from the buyer's own spend data, a live market benchmark, or the current statutory wage index. A supplier-facing agent must not assert any of these as fact. TUPE / employee-liability specifics (protected terms, pension, accrued liabilities) are real UK/EU transfer-of-undertakings mechanics but the buyer must supply the actual, warranted workforce data for the specific contract rather than let the agent estimate it.
