## What drives cost

Financial and insurance cost is not "a price" — it is **risk transfer plus intermediation plus the market cycle**, and each has a different owner you can play. For **insurance**, the premium is fundamentally driven by your own loss ratio (claims paid ÷ premium earned over the look-back period), your exposure base (revenue, headcount, payroll, insured values, fleet size), and where in the **hard/soft market cycle** you renew. In a soft market capacity is abundant and insurers compete on price; in a hard market they push rate, cut limits and widen exclusions. You cannot control the cycle, but you can time renewals, restructure retention, and re-market to exploit it. For **banking and treasury**, cost hides in the spread and the fee stack — FX spread over mid-market, transaction/payment fees, facility/commitment fees on undrawn credit, cash-management and custody pricing — not in the headline rate a relationship manager quotes.

## The key levers

- **Insurance premium: attack the loss ratio narrative.** Present a clean, well-documented claims history and risk-improvement actions (loss control, safety, cyber controls) so the underwriter reprices your actual risk, not a portfolio average. A good story on frequency/severity trend is worth real rate.
- **Move up the retention.** Higher deductible / excess / self-insured retention (SIR) transfers predictable small claims back to you and cuts premium — you stop paying an insurer's margin to swap dollars. Model the retained-loss expected value against the premium saving; only retain what your balance sheet can absorb.
- **Broker: commission vs. fee, and full disclosure.** Demand the broker's remuneration in writing — commission (a % of premium the insurer pays them, which rises with your premium and misaligns incentives) vs. a fixed fee for defined scope. A flat fee aligns them to lower your premium. Ask about contingent/profit commissions and market-derived income too.
- **Multi-line / multi-year structuring.** Bundling lines (property, liability, cyber, D&O) or locking a multi-year deal buys premium stability and can earn a discount — but only trade it for a rate concession, a rate-cap, and a mid-term re-marketing/exit right. Never hand multi-year certainty away for free.
- **Banking: unbundle and benchmark the spread.** Separate FX spread, per-transaction fees, and facility/commitment fees; benchmark FX against mid-market and negotiate a fixed spread, not "market." Compete the wallet — banks cross-subsidise lending with fee income, so use credit as leverage on cash-management pricing.
- **Captive / alternative risk transfer.** For large, stable programmes, a captive or protected cell retains underwriting profit and smooths cycle volatility. It's a strategic structure, not a line-item — flag it for treasury/finance to evaluate.
- **Re-market to create tension.** The single biggest insurance lever is a credible broker-run market submission to multiple carriers. Incumbent renewal quotes soften fast when a competing capacity is visibly at the table.

## Category-specific traps

- **Commission opacity** — broker paid a % of the premium they're meant to reduce; contingent commissions never disclosed.
- **Silent hardening** — renewal holds the premium but quietly cuts limits, raises the retention, or adds exclusions. Compare terms, not just price.
- **Rate vs. premium sleight-of-hand** — a "flat rate" on a growing exposure base still raises your premium.
- **FX "no fee" that hides the spread** — the cost moved into a wider spread over mid-market.
- **Undrawn-facility and commitment fees** priced as boilerplate; auto-renew and profit-share clauses that outlive their value.

## What to trade (give / get)

- **Give contract length (multi-year), get a rate cut + rate cap.** Multi-year is low-cost to you if you keep a re-marketing/exit right; it's high-value stability to the insurer. Trade it — never gift it.
- **Give retention (higher deductible/SIR), get lower premium.** You concede a "term" you can absorb to pull down the price you weight most.
- **Give volume/consolidation (multi-line, whole wallet to one bank), get fee/spread reduction or a rebate.** Certainty of the whole programme is the currency.
- **Give faster payment / annual-in-advance premium, get price.** Cash timing has genuine value to a carrier or bank — a clean logroll.
- **Take a rebate/profit-share when the counterparty won't cut the headline** — a low-loss-ratio profit-share or volume rebate returns the money only if performance holds.

## Engine mapping

- **price** = premium / effective rate, broker fee, FX spread, and the fee stack — minimize; prioritise structure over headline.
- **payment_days** = premium payment timing / DPO — a low-weight give to trade for price.
- **contract_length** = multi-year lock — concede only bundled with a rate cap and re-marketing/exit right.
- **volume** = exposure base, multi-line bundle, banking wallet — convert to rebate, not just discount.
- **rebate** = profit-share / low-loss-ratio return / volume rebate — the face-saving substitute for a rate cut.

## Key levers

- Reprice on your actual loss ratio with a documented claims history and risk-improvement story, not a portfolio average
- Raise deductible / excess / self-insured retention to transfer predictable small claims back and cut premium — sized to your balance sheet
- Force broker remuneration into the open: fixed fee over premium-based commission, and disclose contingent/profit commissions
- Run a credible multi-carrier re-marketing submission to create capacity tension against the incumbent renewal
- Unbundle banking cost — negotiate FX as a fixed spread over mid-market, and attack transaction, facility and commitment fees separately
- Trade multi-year / multi-line lock only for a rate cut, a rate cap, and a mid-term re-marketing / exit right
- Time the renewal and restructure retention to the hard/soft market cycle; compare terms (limits, exclusions) not just premium
- Flag captive / protected-cell / ART structures for large stable programmes to retain underwriting profit and smooth the cycle

## Sourcing discipline

This playbook contains NO invented figures, and a supplier-facing agent must assert none. Every quantitative input a buyer needs must be sourced live at negotiation time: (1) the client's own loss ratio, claims frequency/severity and exposure base — from the buyer's actual loss runs and financials, never estimated; (2) typical or "market" broker commission percentages and whether contingent/profit commissions apply — obtain the specific broker's disclosed remuneration, do not quote a rule-of-thumb %; (3) prevailing insurance rate movement (hard vs. soft market direction and magnitude by line) — from a live market/renewal-rate index at renewal date; (4) current mid-market FX rates and a fair spread benchmark — from a live rate feed, not memory; (5) benchmark banking transaction, facility/commitment and cash-management fees — from a live market benchmark or the buyer's competing bids; (6) deductible/SIR premium-saving trade-offs — must be modelled on the buyer's actual claims distribution, not a generic ratio; (7) captive feasibility thresholds — require actuarial/tax analysis, not a stated minimum premium figure. On EU/German context: any regulatory or disclosure point (e.g. insurance distribution remuneration transparency, banking fee-disclosure duties) is a real obligation area but a specific statutory rate, threshold, or citation must be sourced live with its source and retrieval date before it is stated as fact — an unsourced compliance figure is not a finding.
