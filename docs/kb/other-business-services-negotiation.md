## What drives cost

These services look like commodities but their cost is built from a **unit rate × volume × premium tiers** stack, plus recurring liabilities suppliers hope you never scrutinise. The margin hides in three places: (1) **rush/turnaround premiums** billed as if every job were urgent; (2) **records storage rent** — a monthly per-box/per-GB fee that compounds for the life of the archive, long after the digitisation project ends; and (3) **reuse suppliers don't credit back to you** — translation memory (TM) and print plates/setup you already paid to create. Volume is your strongest lever because these are high-fixed-cost operations: the supplier wants throughput and predictability, and will trade rate for committed volume and a longer framework.

## The key levers (concrete)

- **Decompose the unit rate.** Never accept a blended per-word/per-page/per-unit price. Split into base rate, urgency surcharge, project-management/handling fee, and minimum-charge. Suppliers pad the minimum charge and the PM fee because they're less visible than headline rate.
- **Translation: demand TM leverage.** Insist on tiered rates by match category (new / fuzzy / 100%-match / repetitions). You own the TM — write ownership and portability into the contract so you can leave. Refuse to pay full rate on content you've already translated.
- **Print/records: unbundle setup from run, and storage from retrieval.** Setup/plates amortise over the run; storage rent is separate from retrieval and from destruction. Get the **destruction/exit cost quoted up front** — that's where you get held hostage at end-of-term.
- **Turnaround tiers, not a flat rush.** Define standard/expedited/emergency SLAs with distinct prices, and a rule that only buyer-flagged jobs incur rush. Cap the share of spend that can carry a surcharge.
- **Volume banding with real minimums.** Band the rate down as annual volume rises, but scrutinise any minimum-volume commitment — an unmet minimum is a penalty you pay for demand you didn't have.
- **Framework over spot** for predictable flow (locks rate, kills per-job haggling); keep spot for spiky, one-off work so you're not paying framework overhead on it.
- **Quality/error SLA with teeth:** defined error rate, free correction/re-do, and a service-credit/rebate mechanism — not just an apology.

## Category-specific traps suppliers use

- **Minimum charge inflation** — a high floor per job turns cheap small jobs into expensive ones.
- **Rush-by-default** — everything quoted at expedited tier unless you police it.
- **TM lock-in** — supplier holds the memory, so leaving means re-paying for translations you funded.
- **Storage that outlives value** — you pay rent on boxes/scans no one will ever retrieve; digitise-then-destroy is cheaper but they profit from perpetual storage.
- **Exit/destruction fees** buried in the small print, priced at end-of-term when you have no leverage.
- **Word/page count method** — counting source vs target, or a padded page definition, quietly moves the bill.

## What to trade (give / get)

- **Give payment days, get rate.** Faster payment (shorter payment days) is cheap for you if cash allows and genuinely valuable to these often mid-size suppliers — trade it for unit-rate reduction.
- **Give contract length + volume commitment, get banded rates, TM/reuse discounts, and locked rush caps.** A longer framework with committed volume is your biggest chip.
- **Give predictable, batched work (fewer rush jobs), get a lower standard rate.** Rush avoidance is real cost you can hand over.
- **Take rate as rebate when they won't cut list price:** a volume-triggered retrospective rebate preserves their headline rate while returning cash to you.

## Mapping to the engine's terms

- **price** = the unit rate stack (per-word/page/unit, plus surcharge and minimum-charge — model the effective blended rate).
- **payment days** = your cheapest give; concede here to move price.
- **contract length** = framework term; longer term justifies deeper banding and TM/reuse discounts.
- **volume** = committed annual volume driving the band; also the trigger for rebate.
- **rebate** = retrospective volume rebate and SLA service-credits — the mechanism to recover value when list price won't move.

Logroll: concede **payment days** and **contract length** (low buyer weight if cash and demand are stable) to win **price** and **rebate** (high weight). Never trade away the **error SLA** or **TM ownership** — those are floor terms, not chips.

## Key levers

- Decompose the unit rate into base, urgency surcharge, PM/handling fee, and minimum charge — refuse blended pricing
- Translation: tier rates by TM match category (new/fuzzy/100%/repetitions) and secure TM ownership + portability
- Unbundle print setup/plates from run cost, and records storage rent from retrieval and destruction
- Get the exit/destruction cost quoted up front, before you're locked in at end-of-term
- Define standard/expedited/emergency turnaround tiers with distinct prices and cap the surcharge-eligible spend share
- Band unit rate down by committed annual volume, but stress-test any minimum-volume penalty clause
- Framework for predictable flow (locks rate), spot for spiky one-offs (avoids framework overhead)
- Attach a quality/error SLA with free re-do plus service-credit/rebate, not just remediation

## Sourcing discipline

I asserted NO specific numbers, market rates, or percentages, per the honesty rule. Every figure a buyer agent will eventually need MUST be sourced live before use in supplier-facing prose, specifically: (1) prevailing per-word/per-page/per-unit rates for the language pair, print run, or service — vary by market and must come from live quotes or a rate benchmark, not memory; (2) typical TM match discount ladders (fuzzy/100%/repetition percentages) — real CAT-tool grid conventions exist but the exact discount tiers are commercially negotiated and must be sourced from the actual supplier's grid; (3) records-storage per-box/per-GB monthly rent and destruction/retrieval fees — quote-specific; (4) any rush/expedited surcharge magnitude; (5) statutory context — EU Late Payment Directive (2011/7/EU, recast pending) sets default payment-term limits and interest for B2B/public bodies, and German public procurement (GWB/VgV, UVgO for below-threshold) governs framework use — the exact current limits, thresholds, and interest rates must be verified against the live statute/directive with a retrieval date, not stated from memory; (6) any claim about what suppliers or brokers "typically" charge as margin — must be sourced, never invented. A supplier-facing agent must present none of these as fact without a live source and retrieval date.
