## What drives cost

R&M cost is **labour rate + parts markup + the mix of planned vs reactive work**, wrapped in an SLA. The headline everyone stares at is the hourly labour rate, but the real money sits in three places buyers forget to fix: (a) the **PPM/reactive split** — how much you pre-buy as a fixed planned-maintenance contract vs pay per callout, (b) the **parts markup** applied on top of pass-through spares, and (c) the **SLA regime** — whether response/fix times carry real penalties or are self-reported. A pure day-rate contract with an open "parts at cost plus markup" line and soft SLAs is the supplier's dream: they recover on parts and callouts whatever you squeeze on the rate. Attack the *structure* — decompose labour from parts, fix the PPM scope, and put teeth on the SLA — not just the number.

## The key levers

- **Decompose labour rate and parts markup separately.** Never accept an all-in "per visit" or "per repair" price. Split the fully-loaded labour rate (and its bands: normal / out-of-hours / emergency / travel) from the parts markup on pass-through spares. Both must be named and negotiated on their own line; an all-in figure hides where margin lives.
- **PPM as fixed-price, reactive as a capped rate card.** Buy planned preventive maintenance as a fixed annual price for a defined asset list and task schedule; price reactive/callout work against a pre-agreed **schedule of rates** with call-out fees, response bands, and a cap on markup for variations. Without a rate card, every breakdown is sole-sourced to the incumbent at their price.
- **First-time-fix rate as a headline KPI.** A high first-time-fix rate collapses repeat visits, travel, and downtime — it's often worth more than the labour rate itself. Make it a measured, penalised KPI, not a marketing claim.
- **Response/fix-time SLAs with fee-at-risk.** Separate *response* time from *fix* (restore) time; put a meaningful slice of the contract value at risk against a small number of measurable SLAs, with independent measurement (CMMS logs, not supplier self-scoring) and deductions that are a real remedy, not a token cap.
- **Fixed-price over T&M where the scope is knowable.** T&M rewards slow, parts-heavy work; fixed-price or a priced task catalogue transfers efficiency risk to the supplier. Reserve T&M for genuinely unpredictable works, and cap it.
- **Multi-asset / portfolio consolidation.** Bundling estate-wide or multi-site assets under one contract buys density and one accountability point — treat it as a concession you grant for a rate/markup cut, not a default that dampens line-level tension.
- **Spares stocking, obsolescence, and parts ownership.** Fix who owns and funds consignment/critical spares, agree consumption transparency (open invoices, no margin games on stock), and get an obsolescence-management commitment (notice, last-time-buy, alternatives) so end-of-life parts don't become a captive-pricing event.
- **Self-perform vs subcontract.** Ask what the prime self-delivers vs subcontracts; subcontracted trades carry a second margin. Push high-volume trades to self-delivery or your directly-managed subs to strip the layer.

## Category-specific traps suppliers use

- Low labour rate bid, margin recovered on **parts markup** and callout fees ("buy the rate, win it back on parts").
- **T&M creep** — jobs that could be fixed-price billed by the hour with generous travel and diagnosis time.
- **SLA theatre** — self-reported response times, unmeasurable "fix" definitions, deduction caps that make failure cheap.
- **Callout-fee stacking** — a call-out charge plus minimum-hours plus travel on every reactive visit.
- **Obsolescence as a pricing event** — captive markups on end-of-life spares with no last-time-buy or alternative.
- **PPM padding** — over-frequent planned visits billed as prevention, low first-time-fix so reactive volume stays high.

## What to trade (give / get)

- **Give contract length → get lower labour rate + firmer markup cap.** A longer term lets the supplier amortise mobilisation, training, and spares investment — extract a real rate cut, a capped parts markup, and a mid-term benchmarking/break right in return. Never give length without the benchmarking clause.
- **Give portfolio/volume consolidation → get rate cut + a rebate tier.** Estate density is high-value to them; convert spend certainty into a retro rebate, not just a headline discount you can't verify.
- **Give faster payment days → get price or markup concession.** Faster payment is genuine cash-flow value to labour-and-parts-heavy suppliers; it's a low-weight give for many buyers — a clean logroll for a lower rate or tighter caps.
- **Take rebate over a headline cut** when the supplier resists on rate — a volume- or SLA-triggered rebate protects them on paper and only pays out if the commitment materialises.
- **Hold the line on the SLA penalty regime, parts markup cap, and first-time-fix KPI** — these are your high-weight terms; don't spend concessions there.

## Mapping to the engine's terms

- **price** = the fully-loaded labour rate *and* the parts markup % *and* the PPM fixed price / callout rate card. Prioritise markup cap and PPM/reactive structure over headline hourly rate.
- **payment_days** = DPO; a low-weight give-away to trade for price, markup, or rebate.
- **contract_length** = amortisation lever; concede length only bundled with a benchmarking/market-test right and a break clause.
- **volume** = committed asset portfolio / estate; convert to a rebate, not just a discount.
- **rebate** = the retro/gainshare mechanism tied to volume commitment, SLA/first-time-fix performance, or open-book parts savings.


## Key levers

- Decompose the all-in price into fully-loaded labour rate (with OOH/emergency/travel bands) and a separately-negotiated parts markup — reject undecomposable per-visit rates
- Buy PPM as fixed-price for a defined asset list; price reactive/callout work against a pre-agreed capped schedule of rates
- Make first-time-fix rate a measured, penalised KPI — it collapses repeat visits and often beats the labour rate in value
- Split response-time from fix/restore-time SLAs and put fee-at-risk against them, measured from CMMS logs not supplier self-scoring
- Default to fixed-price or a priced task catalogue over T&M wherever scope is knowable; cap and ring-fence any T&M
- Cap parts markup and demand open invoices on pass-through spares; fix consignment/critical-spares ownership and funding
- Get an obsolescence commitment — notice, last-time-buy, and alternatives — so end-of-life parts aren't captive-priced
- Trade longer contract length for a lower labour rate, capped markup, and a mid-term benchmarking/break right
- Consolidate multi-asset/portfolio volume for a rate cut plus a retro rebate, not just a headline discount
- Strip subcontract margin by pushing high-volume trades to self-delivery or your directly-managed subs

## Sourcing discipline

This playbook contains NO invented figures, and a supplier-facing agent must not assert any of the following as fact — each must be sourced live at negotiation time (from the buyer's own spend/CMMS data, a live market benchmark, or the supplier's warranted data) and cited with a retrieval date: prevailing fully-loaded labour rates and the uplift for out-of-hours / emergency / travel bands (by trade and EU/German region); "typical" or acceptable parts markup percentages on pass-through spares; call-out fee levels and minimum-billable-hours norms; a benchmark or "good" first-time-fix rate; PPM visit frequencies and the fixed annual PPM price for a given asset list; SLA response/fix-time targets and the fee-at-risk / deduction percentages that count as meaningful; rebate thresholds and tiers; and last-time-buy / obsolescence lead times. Any statutory or index figure — the relevant labour/wage indexation index used for multi-year escalation, and any EU/German legal points (e.g. warranty/liability periods, or LkSG/CSDDD supply-chain due-diligence obligations if in scope) — must be pulled from the current official source, not recalled from memory. The actual asset register, spares criticality list, and warranted labour data for the specific contract must come from the buyer/supplier, never be estimated by the agent.
