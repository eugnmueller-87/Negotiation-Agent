## What actually drives cost

List price is theatre. In IT hardware the real cost stack is: OEM cost basis (component/BOM, moves with memory/panel/CPU cycles), the reseller/VAR margin layered on top, plus attach services (warranty, imaging, deployment, disposal) where the fat margin hides. You are almost never negotiating with the OEM — you're negotiating with a reseller/VAR who buys under an OEM "deal reg" or "special bid." That means two prices exist: street margin on hardware (thin, commoditised) and services/attach margin (thick, opaque). Push discount off hardware and suppliers shrug; push on attach and standardisation and you move real money. In the EU add: VAT handling, WEEE/take-back obligations, and Ecodesign/energy-label positioning that suppliers use to justify premium SKUs.

## The key levers (concrete)

- **Volume + standardisation together.** Cutting your SKU count (one laptop line, one monitor, one dock) is worth more than raw volume — it lets the VAR forecast, hold stock, and pull a bigger OEM special bid. Trade a signed SKU-reduction commitment for a deeper standing discount.
- **Buy vs lease/DaaS.** Lease/Device-as-a-Service converts capex to a monthly rate but bundles finance cost, residual-value assumptions, and end-of-term risk (fair-wear clauses, missing-device penalties). Always demand the buy-equivalent price to expose the embedded finance/residual margin. DaaS is a lever, not a gift.
- **Refresh cycle.** Extending 3→4 years cuts annual spend but raises failure/warranty exposure. Use the refresh cadence as a bargaining chip: a committed refresh volume over a fixed term is exactly what earns tiered pricing.
- **Trade-in / asset buyback.** Old fleet has residual value. Force it to be quoted as a *separate line credit*, not baked into a "net" price where you can't see it.
- **Extended warranty vs self-insure.** For large fleets, self-insuring the tail (buying spares, break-fix pool) often beats blanket 4th/5th-year warranty. Price the warranty attach separately and challenge it.
- **Bundling hardware + support.** Suppliers push bundles to hide attach margin. Unbundle to price each, then re-bundle only if the bundle genuinely beats the sum.
- **End-of-life / last-time-buy.** When a model goes EOL, negotiate a last-time-buy at protected pricing plus spares — don't get forced onto a pricier successor mid-cycle.
- **Freight, imaging/staging.** Zero-touch deployment, asset tagging, BIOS config are real services but heavily marked up. Cap or waive freight on volume; competitively benchmark staging per-unit.
- **Price protection.** Component costs swing both ways. Get a clause that passes *decreases* through, not just protects the supplier's *increases* — most one-sided clauses only protect the seller.

## Category traps suppliers use

- "Net" pricing that hides the trade-in credit and the attach margin.
- Bundled DaaS quotes with no buy-equivalent, hiding finance + residual margin.
- One-directional price-protection (increases pass through, decreases don't).
- Free imaging/freight "included" — it was priced into the unit.
- Fair-wear-and-tear and end-of-lease device-loss penalties that claw back the lease saving.
- EOL forcing onto a costlier successor without a last-time-buy option.

## What to trade (give / get)

- **Give payment days** (offer faster pay / shorter terms) **→ get price** or a deeper rebate tier. Cash timing is cheap for a well-funded buyer and valuable to a VAR carrying inventory.
- **Give contract length + committed volume** (multi-year, standardised SKUs) **→ get lower unit price and stronger price protection.**
- **Give forecast accuracy / SKU reduction → get rebate.** Predictability is what unlocks the OEM special bid behind the reseller.
- **Take rebate over headline discount** when spend is uncertain — it self-adjusts to actual volume and is auditable at year-end.

## Mapping to the engine terms

- **price** — hardware unit price after special-bid/deal-reg; keep attach/services on separate lines so this stays clean.
- **payment_days** — your cheap give; concede here to buy price/rebate.
- **contract_length** — multi-year term = the commitment that earns tiered pricing and price protection.
- **volume** — committed units *plus* SKU standardisation; both feed the discount tier.
- **rebate** — back-end volume/growth rebate; prefer when forward volume is uncertain, and use it to bank trade-in credits and overachievement.


## Key levers

- Always demand the buy-equivalent price on any lease/DaaS quote to expose embedded finance and residual-value margin

## Sourcing discipline

Every quantitative figure a buyer agent would want here MUST be sourced live at negotiation time — none are asserted in this playbook because I will not invent them. Specifically: (1) typical/achievable discount-off-list percentages for any OEM or category — these depend on the live OEM deal-reg/special-bid and current channel margin, get them from actual quotes/benchmarks. (2) VAR/reseller margin percentages on hardware vs. attach/services — real but never assume a number; derive from the unbundled quote. (3) Residual-value and finance-rate assumptions inside any lease/DaaS quote — request the supplier's actual figures and the buy-equivalent. (4) Trade-in/buyback residual values — must come from a current asset appraisal, not a rule of thumb. (5) Extended-warranty attach cost and fleet failure rates used to justify self-insuring — pull from the supplier quote and your own fleet history. (6) EU statutory items: current standard/reduced VAT rate for the relevant member state, WEEE/take-back cost obligations, and Ecodesign/energy-label thresholds — verify against the live statute/directive with a retrieval date; do not state a rate from memory. (7) Any 'market average' payment-term (e.g. net-30/45/60) benchmark — confirm against current category data. A supplier-facing agent must assert none of these as fact; if a number is needed it must be quoted from a sourced document or the supplier's own submission.
