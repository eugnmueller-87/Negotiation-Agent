## What actually drives cost

The bill rate you're quoted is **pay rate + agency markup + statutory on-costs**. Suppliers negotiate on the bill rate to keep the split opaque. Your entire job is to **decompose it and negotiate the markup, not the bill rate.** The markup (a percentage or fixed uplift on the worker's pay) is where the agency's margin lives and it is the primary lever. Everything else — placement fees, tenure step-ups, rate-card tiers — is a second cost layer stacked on top.

## The key levers (concrete)

- **Force a "rate-card transparency" / pay-rate-plus-markup model.** Demand pay rate, statutory on-costs, and markup shown as separate line items per skill tier. A blended bill rate hides margin; a decomposed one lets you attack the markup alone. This is the single highest-value move.
- **Cap the markup, not the bill rate.** Negotiate the markup percentage down and fix it, so when statutory wages rise, the agency can't ride the increase for extra margin. Markup should be flat per tier, not proportional windfall.
- **Rate cards by skill tier.** Lock a tiered card (admin / skilled / specialist / management) with a defined markup per tier. Stops "tier creep" — agencies re-classifying a worker up a tier to lift the rate.
- **Tenure / equal-treatment step-ups.** In Germany (AÜG), after a defined hire period the agency worker gets equal pay (equal-treatment), and there's a statutory max assignment duration; in the UK the Agency Worker Regulations trigger equal treatment at a set tenure. Suppliers use the *approaching* step-up as a rotation excuse or a rate-hike trigger. Negotiate who bears the step-up cost and pre-agree the conversion path before the tenure clock forces it.
- **Conversion / placement (temp-to-perm) fees.** These are pure margin and highly negotiable. Push for a **sliding scale that decays to zero** the longer the worker was on assignment (they've already earned margin through markup). Never accept a flat headline placement fee.
- **Volume rebates.** Tie a retrospective rebate to committed annual spend or headcount bands. Structure it so it grows with volume and is paid quarterly, not "reviewed annually."
- **Exclusivity vs multi-supplier.** Exclusivity/sole-supply is your biggest bargaining chip — only give it for a hard markup reduction plus a rebate. Otherwise run a **multi-supplier panel** with a lead agency to keep competitive tension.
- **MSP/VMS.** A Managed Service Provider running a Vendor Management System gives spend visibility and consolidated rate cards. Know who funds it: a "supplier-funded" MSP is paid via a levy on agency margin (fine) — but watch for that levy being passed back into your bill rates.

## Category-specific traps suppliers use

- **Blended bill rate** that hides the markup — refuse it.
- **Tier creep / mis-classification** to a higher-paid skill band.
- **Statutory-increase pass-through with margin attached** — a minimum-wage rise should move pay rate only, not the markup.
- **Rotating workers out just before the tenure/equal-treatment step-up** to reset the clock and dodge equal pay — bad for continuity and often a false economy.
- **Placement fees framed as non-negotiable** — they are among the most negotiable terms in the category.
- **"All-in" rate that buries overtime, holiday pay, and on-costs** — get the on-cost build-up itemized.

## What to trade (give / get)

- **Give** longer contract term and committed volume → **get** a lower fixed markup and a bigger rebate. Agencies value guaranteed utilization highly and weight tenor/volume more than you do.
- **Give** exclusivity or panel-lead status → **get** the best markup tier plus a volume rebate.
- **Give** faster payment (shorter payment days) → **get** markup reduction; agencies fund worker payroll weekly, so DSO relief is genuinely valuable to them and cheap for you to concede if cash allows.
- **Get** a decaying conversion-fee schedule and capped markup in exchange for **giving** a multi-year commitment.

## Mapping to the engine's terms

- **price** (MINIMIZE) = the **markup** on pay rate per skill tier — the real target, not the headline bill rate.
- **payment_days** (MAXIMIZE for the buyer) = a genuine two-way lever here: agencies fund payroll weekly, so *shorter* payment days is something you can concede to buy markup down. Model payment_days as a give-term with high supplier appetite.
- **contract_months** = commitment length; a give-term you trade for markup and rebate.
- **volume** = committed headcount / annual spend that unlocks rebate bands and justifies a lower markup.
- **rebate_pct** (MAXIMIZE) = retrospective volume rebate; a get-term won via exclusivity, term, and volume.
- Conversion/placement fees and tenure step-up cost-bearing are category terms outside the core five — carry them as explicit logrolling give/get terms (low buyer weight, high supplier value on the placement-fee side).

## Key levers

- Decompose the bill rate into pay + on-costs + markup, and negotiate ONLY the markup
- Fix a flat markup per skill tier so statutory wage rises don't hand the agency extra margin
- Lock a tiered rate card to stop tier-creep re-classification into higher bands
- Push conversion/placement fees onto a sliding scale that decays to zero with assignment tenure
- Trade contract length + committed volume for a lower fixed markup and a larger volume rebate
- Concede shorter payment days (agencies fund payroll weekly) to buy the markup down
- Only grant exclusivity/sole-supply for a hard markup cut plus a rebate; else run a multi-supplier panel
- Pre-agree the AÜG/AWR tenure equal-treatment step-up cost and conversion path before the clock forces it

## Sourcing discipline

No specific market figures are asserted anywhere in this playbook, and none should be. Every number a buyer agent needs must be sourced live at negotiation time: (1) the prevailing market markup range for this category/region/skill tier — benchmark it, do NOT assume a percentage; (2) statutory on-cost rates (employer social contributions, holiday pay, sick pay) for the jurisdiction; (3) the exact tenure thresholds and rules for equal-treatment — Germany's AÜG max assignment duration and equal-pay trigger, and the UK Agency Worker Regulations qualifying period — these are set in law and change, so cite the current statute/regulation with a retrieval date rather than a remembered figure; (4) any MSP levy percentage. IR35 is UK off-payroll tax status and is distinct from AWR equal-treatment — do not conflate them if it enters scope. Confirm which jurisdiction's regime applies before invoking AÜG vs AWR/IR35. A supplier-facing message must never state a benchmark, statutory rate, or tenure threshold that was not retrieved and cited.
