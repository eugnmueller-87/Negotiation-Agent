## What drives cost

Freight cost is **linehaul rate + fuel + accessorials + surcharge creep**, and the total is dominated by things buyers forget to fix in the contract. The linehaul (the base rate per lane/mode) is what everyone negotiates; the money leaks through fuel surcharge (BAF/FSC), accessorials, and detention/demurrage — the "below-the-line" items where carriers recover margin they conceded on the headline rate. Cost also swings with **mode** (within road, FTL is cheapest per unit, then LTL, then parcel; for international long-haul, ocean is far cheaper per kg than air — the two axes are independent, not a single ascending line), **lane balance** (backhaul/headhaul imbalance — a carrier's empty return leg is where you win), and **commitment vs. spot** (committed volume buys stability; spot buys flexibility, and each is cheaper than the other at different times of the cycle). Award is usually run as a **tender/RFP** across a lane matrix, which is your single biggest lever: you're not negotiating one number, you're allocating a portfolio.

## The key levers

- **Decompose the price.** Never accept an all-in rate. Split linehaul, fuel, and accessorials so each is negotiated and indexed separately. An all-in rate hides where the margin sits.
- **Cap and formula-fix the fuel surcharge (BAF/FSC).** Tie it to a published index (e.g., a national diesel/bunker index), fix the peg point and the escalation slope, and **cap it**. An uncapped, carrier-defined FSC table is the classic trap — the carrier sets both the trigger and the step size.
- **Attack accessorials line by line.** Detention, demurrage, liftgate, residential, reweigh, layover, redelivery, waiting time. Negotiate free time (detention/demurrage), per-unit rates, and caps. These are pure recovery items.
- **Index-linked pricing over flat annual rates.** Agree the rate = base + published index delta, with a symmetric collar (moves down as well as up). This kills mid-term "market has moved" reopeners.
- **Volume commitment as currency, not a gift.** Commit volume to buy rate — but make the minimum-volume clause soft (band, not a hard floor with penalties) so a demand dip doesn't trigger a shortfall charge.
- **Incoterms decide who pays and who bears risk.** Moving from EXW/FCA toward DAP/DDP shifts cost and risk to the seller but usually raises the unit price; moving toward FCA/FOB lets you control the freight leg and tender it yourself. Choose deliberately — it's a cost-allocation lever, not a formality.
- **Award allocation in the tender.** Split award across carriers (primary/secondary) to keep competitive tension; use the RFP to make carriers bid against their own backhaul lanes.

## Category traps suppliers use

- **Uncapped/self-defined fuel tables** that ratchet the effective rate above the negotiated linehaul.
- **Accessorial ambiguity** — undefined detention free time, "market" demurrage, discretionary reweigh/reclass on LTL.
- **GRI / peak-season surcharges** dropped in mid-term when not contractually excluded or capped.
- **Minimum-volume penalties** framed as your commitment but structured so any shortfall bills at a punitive rate.
- **Spot-rate bait** — a low committed rate that relies on you tendering overflow to their expensive spot desk.

## What to trade (give / get)

Map to the engine terms — **price, payment days, contract length, volume, rebate**:

- **Give contract length, get rate.** A longer term (with index-linked pricing and a cap) is low-cost to you and lets the carrier plan capacity — trade it for a lower linehaul and a firmer FSC cap.
- **Give volume commitment, get rate + a rebate tier.** Route committed volume to the carrier's strong (backhaul) lanes — high value to them, and you'd move it anyway.
- **Give payment days, get price.** Faster payment (fewer payment days) is real cash-flow value to an asset-heavy carrier; concede it to pull down the linehaul or accessorial caps. This is a low-weight term for many buyers and high-value to the supplier — a clean logroll.
- **Take rebate over headline discount** when the carrier resists a rate cut: a volume-triggered rebate protects them on paper while returning the same money to you, and only pays out if the volume materializes.
- **Hold the line on FSC cap and accessorials** — these are your high-weight terms; don't spend concessions there.

## Engine mapping

Price = decomposed linehaul + capped FSC (minimize). Contract length and volume are the currency you concede to move price. Payment days are a low-weight give with high supplier value. Rebate is the face-saving substitute for a headline rate cut, tied to committed volume.

## Key levers

- Decompose the all-in rate into linehaul + fuel + accessorials and negotiate/index each separately
- Cap the fuel surcharge and peg it to a published diesel/bunker index with a fixed formula, never a carrier-defined table
- Negotiate every accessorial line by line — detention/demurrage free time, per-unit rates, and hard caps
- Use index-linked pricing with a symmetric collar to block mid-term 'market moved' reopeners
- Trade longer contract length (with a rate cap) for a lower linehaul and firmer FSC cap
- Route committed volume to the carrier's backhaul/strong lanes to win rate for volume you'd ship anyway
- Concede faster payment days (high value to asset-heavy carriers) to pull down price and accessorial caps
- Take a volume-triggered rebate when the carrier won't cut the headline rate, and split tender award primary/secondary to keep tension

## Sourcing discipline

This playbook deliberately contains NO specific numbers — no benchmark rates, no typical FSC caps, no detention free-time hours, no markup percentages, no rebate thresholds. Every such figure must be sourced live before use: benchmark the current linehaul rate per lane/mode against a live rate index or recent bids; look up the current published diesel/bunker index value and its historical peg for the FSC formula; pull current market detention/demurrage free-time norms and per-hour/per-day rates for the relevant mode and region; and confirm current peak-season/GRI surcharge levels from the carrier's published tariff. A supplier-facing agent must not assert any of these as fact — state them as "to be benchmarked" and cite the live source and retrieval date.
