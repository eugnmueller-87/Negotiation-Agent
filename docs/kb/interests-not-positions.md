# Focus on interests, not positions

A **position** is what a party says they want ("we need a 5% discount"). An **interest**
is *why* they want it (hit a budget number, show a saving to Finance, match a competitor,
de-risk a renewal). Positions collide head-on and produce a win-lose tug of war; interests
often turn out to be compatible, and satisfying them opens moves a positional fight hides.

The move is to ask **why** and **why not** behind every stated position — both yours and
theirs. A supplier holding firm on list price may really be protecting a quarter-end
revenue number; that interest is served just as well by a shorter-term deal, a case study,
or a faster signature — none of which cost you on price. A buyer demanding a lower unit
price may really need predictable total cost; a price cap or a volume tier serves that
interest without the supplier conceding headline rate.

## Why this matters for a procurement agent

Behind most price positions sit interests you can trade against:

- **Revenue timing** → offer an earlier signature or a multi-year commit.
- **Reference / logo** → offer a case study or a referenceable deal.
- **Risk of churn** → offer a longer term for a better rate.
- **Internal budget line** → shift value between one-off and recurring cost.

When you find an interest the other side values highly and you value lightly, you have the
raw material for a trade. That is the bridge to [[options-for-mutual-gain]] and, in this
engine, to **logrolling**: concede on a low-weight term the supplier's interest values,
to hold a high-weight term yours does.

## Guardrail

Interests are inferred, not known. State them as hypotheses to test in the conversation,
never as asserted facts about the counterparty's internal state. In this system the buyer
agent's belief about supplier appetite is exactly that — a *belief* (numbers only), and it
never overrides the deterministic mandate. See [[batna-and-reservation-value]] and
[[objective-criteria]].
