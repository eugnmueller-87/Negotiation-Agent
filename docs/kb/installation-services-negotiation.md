## What drives cost

Installation cost is **labour hours × loaded day-rate + materials/consumables + risk premium + subcontractor markup + preliminaries (prelims: access, welfare, plant, supervision)**. The single biggest cost driver is not the headline rate — it is **who owns the unknowns**. Suppliers price site-condition uncertainty, access risk, and scope gaps as fat contingency baked into the number. Whoever carries that risk pays for it. The second driver is **productivity assumptions**: hours per unit installed, assumed clear access, assumed working hours. A supplier who assumes obstructed access and out-of-hours work quietly inflates the base.

## The key levers

- **Fixed-price (lump sum) vs day-rate.** Push for fixed-price against a *defined* scope and drawings — it forces the supplier to own their productivity and contingency. Accept day-rate only for genuinely undefinable work, and cap it (rate card + a not-to-exceed ceiling + your right to instruct a stop). Suppliers prefer day-rate precisely because it transfers scope risk back to you.
- **Site-condition / scope-risk allocation.** The classic trap is a low base price plus wide-open "unforeseen conditions" and "unless otherwise stated" carve-outs that become variations later. Demand a **site survey before pricing**, a documented list of assumptions/exclusions, and that the supplier is deemed to have satisfied themselves on access and conditions. Convert as many named risks as possible into the fixed price.
- **Snagging / defect-liability period (DLP).** In EU/DE the statutory warranty (Gewährleistung) for building/installation work under the German BGB / VOB/B is a real floor you should not negotiate below — the exact period must be sourced live, not asserted here. Push the *contractual* snagging and make-good obligations, response times for defects, and that final acceptance (Abnahme) starts the clock, not practical completion.
- **Retention.** Withhold a retention percentage released in two tranches (at acceptance and at end of DLP) as security for snagging — real leverage. The retention %/split are a live-sourced/market-driven negotiation point.
- **Out-of-hours / premium labour.** Insist premium rates apply only to hours *you instruct* out-of-hours, at a pre-agreed uplift, not as a default on the whole job. Reject blanket "shift premium" loaded into the base.
- **Phasing & access constraints.** Access and phasing are where suppliers claim disruption/standing-time. Pin down access windows, sequencing, and who bears standing-time in the contract. Give realistic access to buy a lower price; vague access invites padded contingency.
- **Make-good & reinstatement.** Define make-good scope explicitly (cores, penetrations, finishes) so it is in the price — otherwise it returns as a variation.
- **H&S / compliance cost.** RAMS, CDM-equivalent duties, permits, and site-safety are the supplier's obligation and should be in the base, not a line-item surcharge. Confirm compliance is priced in, not bolted on.
- **Subcontractor markup.** Ask for the markup on subcontracted packages to be disclosed and capped; require open-book on subbed work above a threshold.
- **Tie to equipment supply vs standalone.** Bundling install with the equipment supplier can cut interface risk and give a single throat to choke — but kills price tension. Keep install **competitively tenderable** even if you buy the kit from one vendor, or use the equipment volume as leverage on the install rate.

## Category-specific traps

- "Provisional sums" and "to be confirmed on site" — a blank cheque; force fixed pricing or a capped allowance.
- Variation/change-order margins far above base margin — agree the variation markup up front.
- Retention released too early, or acceptance signed before snagging closes.
- Prelims and standing-time recharged for access delays you didn't cause.

## What to trade (give / get)

- **Give payment days, get price.** Faster payment (fewer PAYMENT_DAYS) is cheap for you if you're liquid and materially eases a contractor's cash flow — trade it for a lower PRICE or tighter DLP.
- **Give volume/pipeline, get rate.** Committing a programme of sites (VOLUME_UNITS) or a longer framework (CONTRACT_MONTHS) justifies a lower installed rate and standing crews.
- **Give access certainty, get contingency back.** Firm access windows and clean drawings let the supplier drop padded risk from PRICE.
- **Get, don't give, on retention and DLP** — these are your defect security; concede price elsewhere before weakening them.

## Mapping to the engine terms

- **price** → installed lump sum or day-rate (the risk-loaded number; your primary MINIMIZE term).
- **payment_days** → your cheapest concession currency; trade down against price.
- **contract_months** → framework length; longer term ↔ lower rate, standing crews, priority.
- **volume_units** → number of sites/units/installs committed; the discount justifier.
- **rebate_pct** → volume rebate or retention-style holdback proxy; ties realized spend to price relief.


## Key levers

- Fixed-price against defined drawings — make the supplier own productivity and contingency; cap and ceiling any day-rate
- Force a pre-price site survey and a written assumptions/exclusions list so unforeseen-conditions can't become variations
- Hold retention released in tranches (acceptance + end of defect-liability period) as snagging security
- Restrict premium/out-of-hours rates to hours YOU instruct at a pre-agreed uplift — reject shift premium in the base
- Pin access windows, phasing, and standing-time liability in the contract; trade firm access for dropped contingency
- Put make-good, RAMS/H&S, permits, and reinstatement inside the base price, not as surcharges
- Disclose and cap subcontractor markup; open-book on subbed packages above a threshold
- Agree the variation/change-order markup up front and ban open 'provisional sums' — cap or fix any allowance

## Sourcing discipline

Nothing quantitative was asserted as fact. Every figure a buyer or a supplier-facing agent would need MUST be sourced live, not invented: (1) the German/EU statutory warranty/Gewährleistung period for construction and installation work under the BGB and VOB/B (differs by regime and work type) — do not state a year count without a legal source and retrieval date; (2) typical/standard retention percentage and its release split; (3) benchmark installed day-rates, loaded labour rates, and hours-per-unit productivity for the specific trade and region; (4) market subcontractor markup percentages; (5) out-of-hours/shift premium uplift percentages; (6) typical variation/change-order margins; (7) defect-liability-period lengths that are contractual rather than statutory. The distinction between statutory minimums (Gewährleistung / VOB/B — a floor set by law) and negotiable contractual terms is real but the specific durations and rates must be confirmed against current statute and live market data before any number reaches a counterparty.
