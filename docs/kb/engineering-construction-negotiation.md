## What actually drives cost

You are buying professional judgment and liability-bearing hours, not a product. Cost is driven by (a) the **fee basis** — percentage-of-construction-cost, lump-sum, or time-charge — (b) the **defined scope and deliverable set**, (c) the **risk the firm carries** (indemnity levels, liability caps, IP), and (d) **change-order leakage**. The single biggest lever is fee basis. Percentage-of-cost fees perversely reward the designer when the build gets more expensive — the incentive is backwards. Lump-sum on a *frozen, fully-defined scope* aligns the firm to your budget; time-charge with a cap suits genuinely undefined early work. Attack the structure before the rate.

## The key levers

- **Fee basis conversion.** Push percentage-of-construction-cost toward lump-sum once scope is defined. If a percentage stays (common under HOAI-style stage fee logic in Germany), fix the *construction cost basis* early and cap it, so scope growth doesn't auto-inflate the fee. HOAI fee tables are now advisory, not binding minimums, since the 2019 ECJ ruling and the 2021 reform — fees are freely negotiable. Do not accept "HOAI mandates this rate" as fact.
- **Stage/phase gating (RIBA Plan of Work / HOAI Leistungsphasen).** Tie fee to defined work stages (RIBA 0–7 / HOAI LP 1–9) with a go/no-go and a payment release at each gate. This lets you exit after concept without buying detailed design, and stops front-loaded billing.
- **Scope and deliverable specification.** The costliest ambiguity is what's *in* the base fee. Enumerate deliverables per stage; name what is excluded (surveys, specialist sub-consultants, BIM level, revisions count). Every unlisted "extra" is otherwise sole-sourced to the incumbent at their price.
- **Change-order control.** Require a priced variation rate card agreed up front, a written instruction before any chargeable work, and a cap on cumulative variations before re-approval. "Design development" must not be a billable variation for the firm's own coordination errors.
- **Professional indemnity (PI) insurance.** Specify a required level and that it is maintained *on an each-and-every-claim basis* and for a run-off period after completion — for latent design defects that surface years later. Do not name a figure as market-standard; set it to project risk and verify the certificate.
- **Liability caps.** Firms push to cap liability at a multiple of fee (or at the PI level). A cap set at the fee is often far below the loss a defective design can cause. Negotiate the cap against project value and carve out fraud, negligence, and IP infringement from any cap.
- **IP / design ownership + licence.** Default to you owning or holding an irrevocable licence to use, reproduce, and modify the design for the completed and future works. Firms retain copyright by default in many EU jurisdictions — secure at minimum a broad licence surviving termination.
- **Novation.** On design-and-build, the design team is often novated to the contractor. Insist on a pre-agreed novation deed and a duty-of-care/collateral warranty back to you, so you keep a direct claim.
- **Payment, retention, milestones.** Stage payments against accepted deliverables, not calendar. Hold retention (a withheld % released on completion/defects-liability expiry) as leverage for close-out.

## Category-specific traps

- "Percentage of cost is standard" — it is negotiable; the percentage basis rewards overspend.
- Front-loaded stage fees (heavy on early concept phases) so you've paid most of the fee before you can judge the design.
- Liability capped at the fee while PI cover is far higher — you're funding insurance you can't reach.
- Excluded sub-consultants (geotech, fire, façade) surfacing as costly variations.
- Copyright retained silently, blocking reuse or a follow-on firm.
- Vague "abortive work" and "additional services" clauses that convert coordination fixes into variations.

## What to trade (give low-value, get high-value)

- **Give contract length / a framework of future projects (volume) → get a lump-sum rate cut and better IP/liability terms.** Pipeline certainty is real value to a practice.
- **Give faster payment days → get a fee reduction or a higher liability cap.** Professional firms are cash-sensitive.
- **Give a reasonable, project-scaled liability cap → get lower fee and full IP assignment.** Un-carved-out uncapped liability just buys a padded risk premium.
- **Give staged go/no-go commitment → get lower per-stage rates** in exchange for pipeline visibility.

## Mapping to the engine's terms

- **price** = the fee — its *basis* (%-of-cost / lump-sum / time-charge) and level. Prioritise basis and stage-gating over headline number.
- **payment_days** = when stage payments and retention release; a cash lever to trade for price or a higher liability cap.
- **contract_length** = single project vs multi-project framework; concede duration only for a rate cut plus IP/liability gains.
- **volume** = committed project pipeline; convert to framework pricing, not a one-off discount.
- **rebate** = retention and any performance/gainshare hold-back released on defect-free close-out.

## Sourcing discipline

No figures are asserted as fact anywhere in this playbook — every one must be sourced live at negotiation time. Specifically: (1) HOAI stage-fee percentages and fee-table values — HOAI fees are advisory/freely negotiable since the 2019 ECJ ruling (C-377/17) and the 2021 HOAI reform; do NOT quote a table rate as a binding minimum, and confirm current legal status live. (2) Any "typical" or "market-standard" percentage-of-construction-cost fee — must come from live market benchmark or the buyer's own spend data. (3) Required PI insurance amounts and run-off period length — set to project risk from a live source / the buyer's insurance advisor, never a quoted default. (4) Liability cap multiples of fee — no standard number; derive from project value. (5) Retention percentage and defects-liability period length — contract/jurisdiction-specific, source per deal. (6) Variation rate-card day rates. (7) RIBA Plan of Work stage numbering (0–7) and HOAI Leistungsphasen (1–9) are named as real frameworks, but confirm the current published versions before citing specific stage content. A supplier-facing agent must not assert any of these as fact.
