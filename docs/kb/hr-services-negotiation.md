## What drives cost

HR services vendors price on a **PEPM (per-employee-per-month) × headcount × months** base, plus one-time **implementation/setup**, plus per-module and per-transaction add-ons. Because PEPM multiplies across your whole workforce and the full contract term, a small unit concession is a large absolute win — PEPM is the term that actually moves total contract value. Suppliers know this, so they defend PEPM hardest and give ground on things that look generous but cost them little (implementation discounts, a free module trial, "waived" fees that reappear at renewal).

The second cost driver is **switching friction**: once your payroll/HRIS is live, migrating off is painful, so vendors quote thin on year 1 and recover margin at renewal and through uplift clauses. Your leverage is highest **before signature** and at **renewal with a credible alternative** — nowhere else.

## The key levers (concrete)

- **Unbundle then rebundle.** Force a line-item quote: core payroll, HRIS, benefits admin, time & attendance, reporting, support tier, each priced separately. Vendors bundle to hide which modules carry margin. Once unbundled, drop modules you don't use and rebundle only what you need — the bundle discount should survive, not the padding.
- **Split implementation from recurring and cap it.** Implementation is a one-time sunk cost the vendor wants amortized into PEPM. Negotiate it as a **fixed, milestone-based fee with acceptance criteria**, and tie final payment to go-live sign-off. Never accept "implementation billed at time & materials."
- **Kill data-migration as a profit center.** Migrating your historical employee/payroll data should be **in-scope of implementation, not a surprise line item**. Demand it be quoted upfront and fixed.
- **Benefits-admin carrier economics.** If the vendor also brokers or administers benefits, ask directly whether they receive **carrier commissions, overrides, or bonuses**. That's a hidden revenue stream — use it to press the admin PEPM down, or require fee transparency / commission offset.
- **Price protection, not just price.** Win a **capped annual uplift** (or index-linked cap) for the whole term. An open renewal clause lets the vendor claw back every year-1 concession.
- **Offboarding and data portability as a signed term.** Get contractual **data export in a standard format at no incremental fee, plus a transition-assistance period** at pre-agreed rates. Without it, the vendor owns your exit and prices accordingly at renewal.
- **SLA on payroll accuracy with teeth.** A payroll-accuracy / on-time-filing SLA with **service credits** is standard and cheap to ask for; a vendor refusing credits is signaling low confidence.

## Category-specific traps

- **"Free implementation" financed through a longer lock.** The setup discount is recovered via a multi-year commit and renewal uplift. Price the whole term, not year 1.
- **PEPM that only applies at a headcount tier you'll dip below.** Confirm the rate holds across your realistic headcount range; get a **true-down**, not just a true-up.
- **Per-transaction fees** (off-cycle runs, W-2/year-end, garnishments, corrections) buried in an appendix. Get the full fee schedule and cap or waive high-frequency ones.
- **Module bundling where one sticky module subsidizes the rest** — you can't drop it later without repricing everything.

## What to trade (give to get)

Give the vendor the things they value that cost you little; take PEPM and exit protection.

- **Give a longer term / commitment; get lower PEPM + a firm uplift cap + a mid-term exit-for-cause.** Length is your cheapest currency — but only trade it for locked economics, never a bare discount.
- **Give faster payment (shorter payment days); get PEPM or implementation reduction.** Improved cash timing is real value to the vendor.
- **Give committed/growing headcount (volume); get a lower PEPM tier and a rebate on overage.**
- **Give a reference/case-study or a phased rollout; get waived migration and capped implementation.**

## Mapping to the engine's terms

- **price →** PEPM (primary) and the fixed implementation fee (secondary). This is the high-weight term for the buyer.
- **payment days →** invoicing terms; concede faster payment to buy PEPM down.
- **contract length →** the multi-year lock. High supplier value, low buyer cost *if* paired with an uplift cap and an exit clause — the classic logrolling give.
- **volume →** committed headcount / seats; drives the PEPM tier.
- **rebate →** growth rebate or overage credit as headcount scales, and service-credit SLA on payroll accuracy.

Trade length, payment days, and volume (low buyer weight, high supplier value) to protect PEPM and offboarding rights (high buyer weight).

## Key levers

- Force a line-item PEPM + per-module + per-transaction quote before discussing any bundle discount
- Cap implementation as a fixed milestone fee tied to go-live acceptance; never T&M
- Fold data migration into implementation scope at a fixed price — refuse it as a separate line item
- Demand disclosure of benefits-carrier commissions/overrides and use them to press admin PEPM down
- Win a capped annual uplift for the full term so year-1 concessions can't be clawed back at renewal
- Sign offboarding data-export in standard format at no fee, plus a priced transition-assistance period
- Attach a payroll-accuracy / on-time-filing SLA with service credits
- Trade contract length, faster payment, and committed headcount to buy PEPM down and lock exit rights

## Sourcing discipline

This playbook deliberately contains NO benchmark numbers — no market PEPM range, no "typical" implementation-to-recurring ratio, no standard uplift-cap percentage, no carrier-commission rate, no SLA-credit magnitude. Every one of those would need to be sourced live for the specific market, headcount band, and vendor before a buyer agent asserts it. Any place the agent wants to say "the market rate is X" or "vendors typically mark up Y%," it must first pull a current benchmark (recent comparable quotes, an advisory/benchmarking source, or an RFP field) and cite it with a retrieval date — it must never state a figure from memory to the supplier. Whether a given vendor actually earns carrier commissions is also vendor-specific and must be confirmed by direct disclosure, not assumed.
